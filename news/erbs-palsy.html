Erb's palsy

Overview of birth injuries and their causes

Erb's palsy, also known as brachial plexus birth palsy, is a condition that affects the nerves in a baby's shoulder, leading to weakness or paralysis in the arm. This condition is typically caused by injury to the brachial plexus during childbirth.


There are several factors that can contribute to the development of Erb's palsy. One common cause is when excessive force is applied to the baby's head and neck during delivery, leading to stretching or tearing of the nerves in the brachial plexus. This can occur if the baby's shoulder becomes stuck behind the mother's pubic bone during a difficult delivery, known as shoulder dystocia.


Another potential cause of Erb's palsy is when excessive traction is applied to the baby's head and neck during delivery. This can occur if a healthcare provider pulls too hard on the baby's head while trying to deliver them from the birth canal.


In some cases, Erb's palsy can also be caused by abnormal positioning of the baby in the womb, such as breech presentation or shoulder presentation. These positions can increase the risk of nerve damage during delivery.


It is important for healthcare providers to be aware of these risk factors and take appropriate precautions during delivery to minimize the chance of injury to the brachial plexus. Early recognition and treatment of Erb's palsy are essential for maximizing recovery and preventing long-term complications.


Overall, understanding the causes of Erb's palsy can help healthcare providers take steps to prevent this condition and provide timely intervention for affected infants. By promoting awareness and education about this condition, we can work towards improving outcomes for babies born with Erb's palsy.
